Gradient Descent Based Algorithms

Optimization algorithms works well when we try to optimize a convex function. On the other hand, when we try to optimize a non-convex function with a saddle point, the situation is different. Gradient Descent based optimization algorithms are successful most of the time with near a saddle point, except Steepest Descent. When we are trying to optimize a function, the starting point that we choose is decisive. When we working with a non-convex function with a saddle point, some starting points can cause our optimization algorithm to stuck in a local minimum. Let's look at some examples.

Steepest Descent

Steepest Descent behaves different than the other Gradient Descent based algorithms. It calculates the lowest possible slope and choose a direction. In that direction, it moves until it finds the smallest point. After that, it iteratively choose direction and find a minimum in that direction. This iteration will continue until it finds the global minimum.

Steepest Descent works well in convex functions. But in non-convex functions, it will stuck on local minimum or it goes to infinite, based on the starting point.
